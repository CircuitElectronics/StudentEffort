linear-nonlinear#

saman_varasteh

Linearity and linear systems are important in science and engineering for two reasons:

  1. Linear systems are easy to think about – at least when compared to nonlinear systems!

  2. A great many systems are approximately linear if we look at them the right way.

Linear systems give rise to a rich ground of understanding and are natural to think about and design, even when the underlying physics is nonlinear. Furthermore, we can and often do design electronic and other systems to provide linear control of something that is nonlinear underneath.

Perhaps most importantly, a great deal of nonlinear systems can be analyzed by breaking them down into a number of locally linear systems,Linearity and linear systems are important in science and engineering for two reasons:

level1:A Single Linear Equation#

Consider the slope-intercept form of a line in the 2D x-y plane:

\(y = mx +b\)

in this euqation x is independent variable and y is variable. and we can find easily dependent variable with doing multiplication and addition. the standard form of line equation :

\( Ax + By = C \)

this form is very nice and useful because all constant on right side and all variable on the left side . in this form for solving equation we can use equivalent matrix equation form :

\(Gw=H\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} A & B \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} C \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

for example :

\(y=3x+4\)

in this example first we transform equation to standard form:

\(-3x + y = 4\)

after this transformation we convert it to matrix equation :

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

this equation show a line not a point on it. and for this equation there are many solution , cause there are infinite point on a line, and that is means \( \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \) ,\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix} \),\( \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} \) and too many other point can be a solution. and for isolating the solution we can give some point and then solve the equation . for instance how to find value of y when x=7.

for find the answer we can do some multipication and addition but a good way for finding it is using matrix equation form:

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

In the case above, the only solution is: $$ \mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix} x \ y \end{bmatrix}#

\begin{bmatrix} 7 \ 25 \end{bmatrix} $$

now let’s solve an equation :

Consider the slope-intercept form of a line in the 2D x-y plane:

\(5x+ 4=3y\)

find the x while \(y=4\)

first step: convert to standard form :

\( 5x - 3y= -4\)

second step: convert to matrix form;

\(Gw=H\)

third step : solve equation:

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[5, -3], [0, 1]])
B = np.array([-4, 4])

x = np.linalg.solve(A, B)

print(f"x = {x[0]}")
print(f"y = {x[1]}")

x = 1.6
y = 4.0

Level 2: Multiple Linear Equations#

now let’s look at this simple electronic circuit with this variable:

\(V_1=10\)

\(I_2=\frac 1{20} v_1\)

\(I_3=3\)

\(I_1=I_2 - I_3\)

import schemdraw
import schemdraw.elements as elm

with schemdraw.Drawing() as d:
    d.config(unit=5)
    V1 = elm.SourceV().label('10V')
    R1 = elm.Line().right()
    elm.Dot() 
    d.push()
    R2 = elm.Resistor().down().label('20Ω', loc='bot', rotate=True)
    elm.CurrentLabel(top=False, ofst=.3).at(R2).label('$I_2$')
    elm.Dot()
    d.pop()
    L1 = elm.Line()
    I1 = elm.SourceI().down().label('3A', loc='bot').reverse()
    L2 = elm.Line().tox(V1.start)
    elm.LoopCurrent([R1,R2,L2,V1], pad=1.25).label('$I_1$')
    elm.LoopCurrent([R1,I1,L2,R2], pad=1.25).label('$I_3$').reverse()

this system design to solve simply and you can solve equation by doing some multipication and addition.

\(V1=10\)

\(I_2=\frac 12\)

\(I_3=3\)

\(I_1=-2.5\)

From this point on, we will refer to the independent variables as inputs and the dependent variables as outputs. let’s consider \(V_1=10\) as the only input in the circuit ,what happend if we change \(V_1\) to 11 , 9 or etc on outputs. let’s consider we change amount of input by \(\Delta x\) what happend to \(I_1\) ,\(I_2\),\(I_3\) . let’s check:

\(V1=10 + \Delta x\)

\(I_2=\frac 1{20} (10+ \Delta x)\)

\(I_3=3\)

\(I_1=\frac 1{20} (10+ \Delta x) - 3\)

and the whole changes on inputs and outputs are as follows :

\(\Delta V1=\Delta x\)

\(\Delta I_2=\frac 1{20} \Delta x\)

\(\Delta I_3=0\)

\(\Delta I_1=\frac 1{20} \Delta x\)

With all these deltas, we now know that if we increase \(V_1\) by a little bit, we can say how that is going to affect all the other variables in the system.

We can and will consider systems with multiple inputs and multiple outputs, but the overall linear system concept is that:

1)We can solve systems of linear equations.

2)We can choose our independent variables (inputs) and dependent variables (outputs).

3)All the equations are linear, so every input-output relationship will be linear too.

In matrix form we can write:

\(y=Ax+b\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_M \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1N} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2N} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{M1} & a_{M2} & \cdots & a_{MN} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_N \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ \vdots \\ b_M \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

in this equation x is the inputs(independent variable) and y is outputs (dependent variable),based on this equation let’s rewrite equation for electronic circuit:

\[\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} I_1 \\ I_2 \\ I_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac1{20}\\ \frac1{20} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -3\\ 0\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

you can see answer in below code

import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1/20], [1/20], [0]])
x = 10
B = np.array([-3, 0, 3])


I = A.flatten() * x + B
print(I)

but the main purpose of this level and the whole project is convert a non_linear system to linear system . at level 1 in Linearization we try to convert equations to standard matrix form:

\(Gw=H\)

now let’s change the above matrix to standard form of matrix:

\(V_1=10\)

\(I_1 - I_2 + I_3 = 0\)

\(I_2 - \frac 1{20} v_1= 0\)

\(I_3=3\)

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\begin{split} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & 1\\ - \frac 1{20} & 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ I_1 \\ I_2 \\ I_3 \end{bmatrix} =\end{split}\\\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} 10\\ 0\\ 0\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]
import numpy as np

A = np.array([
    [1, 0, 0, 0],
    [0, 1, -1, 1],
    [-1/20, 0, 1, 0],
    [0, 0, 0, 1]
])


b = np.array([10, 0, 0, 3])


x = np.linalg.solve(A, b)

print("Solution x:")
print(x)

Computers are really good at quickly solving this linear equation (if G is square and certain other conditions are met) even if there are 100 or 1000 simultaneous equations and unknowns.

Level 5: A Single Differential Equation#

in level 3 we talk about memoryles system ,but in many real systems we need some differential equation like \(\frac {dx}{dt}\) and cause in differential equation we need to now the value of last time so our systems should have memory. Taking a derivative is a linear operator,this should be weird . for example:

\(\frac {dx}{dt} sin(t)=cos(t)\)

Since \(sin(t)\) and \(cos(t)\) aren’t proportional to each other, how are they possibly linear?

based on definition of linearity in function :

if F is linear then:

\(F(a.x(t) + b.y(t)) = a.F(x(t)) + b.F(y(t))\)

A linear operator scales up and down in size with a constant term, and it distributes linearly to a sum.

A derivative follows these properties, so a derivative is a linear operator.

The big idea here is that when we talk about linear systems, we’re not talking about just mapping an input value (a single number) to an output value (a different number). We’re really talking about something that takes an input signal (a function of time) and gives an output signal (a different function of time). We’ve defined linearity more rigorously, and any linear system as we’ve previously defined in terms of \(Gw=H\) is a linear system regardless of how we define inputs or outputs within \(w\)

Level 6: Multiple Differential Equations#

by combine level 2 and level 5 , we can take the general matrix equation form :

\(Gw=H\)

and if we extend it :

\(Ax+B\dot x=H\)

in this equation \(\dot x\) is the matrix of derviation of each individual component :

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\begin{split} \dot x = \begin{bmatrix} \frac {dx}{dt} x_1(t) \\ \frac {dx}{dt} x_2(t)\\ \vdots \\ \frac {dx}{dt} x_n(t)\end{split}\\\end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}\end{align} \]

this is the most general form of matrix equation , and if we don’t need to use all derivative component we can set amount of B to 0.

To solve the equation \(Gw = H \) uniquely, we need a square \( N \times N \) matrix \( G \), meaning the number of unknowns must equal the number of linearly independent variables. However, when differential equations are involved, there’s a complication: both \( x_i \) and its derivative \( \dot{x}_i \) are unknowns.

If the original matrices \( A \) and \( B \) are each \( N \times N \), the combined matrix \( G = [A \, B] \) becomes \( N \) rows by \( 2N \) columns, leading to more unknowns than equations. This means the system is no longer square, reflecting the need for additional information (e.g., \( x \) to compute \(\dot{x}\) or vice versa).

To solve a Kth-order linear differential equation, K additional constraints (e.g., initial or boundary values) are needed. These constraints select a unique solution from the infinitely many possibilities allowed by the differential equation.

The big idea here is that we can easily modify our approach to systems of linear equations to incorporate linear differential equations, which occur in electronics every time there’s a capacitor or inductor.
